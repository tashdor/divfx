# Notes for v1.1

This page collects everything identified during the wiki port that should be addressed in a
v1.1 release. It is organised into three tiers:

- **Factual errors** — statements that were wrong when written, or are wrong now. These should
  be fixed regardless of how much revision the rest of the document receives.
- **Out of date** — statements that were accurate in Fall 2017 but no longer describe the
  industry.
- **Gaps** — material a 2026 edition needs that the 2017 edition does not cover at all.

Every claim below was verified against primary sources in July 2026. Where a finding is
uncertain, that is stated explicitly.

!!! note "Nothing on this page has been silently applied to the chapters"
    The chapter text still reads as v1.0.1 did, minus copy-editing. Where a passage is affected
    by something below, an inline callout links here. That keeps the port faithful and makes the
    editorial decisions visible rather than buried.

## Factual errors

### H.264 / H.265 bit-depth claims

> **In [Common Working Formats](working-formats.md#h264-h265-hevc):** "H.264 is limited to
> 8 bits per channel, while H.265 is limited to 10 bits."

This is wrong as a statement about the standards. Both define higher-bit-depth professional
profiles:

- **H.264** defines High 10 (up to 10-bit), High 4:2:2 (10-bit), and High 4:4:4 Predictive
  (Hi444PP) — up to **14 bits** per sample, 4:4:4, with lossless coding modes.
- **HEVC** defines Main 12 (8–12 bit) and Main 4:4:4 12 (8–12 bit, up to 4:4:4), with Range
  Extensions reaching 16-bit.

8-bit and 10-bit are *common deployment ceilings* set by hardware decoders and streaming
platforms, not format limits.

**Suggested replacement:** "In practice, consumer H.264 and H.265 deliverables are 8-bit and
10-bit 4:2:0 respectively, because that is what hardware decoders and streaming platforms
support — but both standards define higher-bit-depth, higher-chroma professional profiles
(H.264 High 10 / Hi444PP up to 14-bit; HEVC Main 12 / Main 4:4:4 12) used in mezzanine and
archival contexts."

### Rec.709 and the OETF/EOTF conflation

> **In [Color Spaces](color.md#display-color-spaces) and
> [An Overview of Digital Intermediates](digital-intermediates.md#display-referred-imagery):**
> Rec.709 described as having "an implied approximate gamma of 2.4".

BT.709 specifies only an **OETF** — the camera-side encoding curve, piecewise with a 0.45 power
segment — along with primaries and matrix coefficients. It defines **no EOTF**. The display-side
transfer function was left undefined until **ITU-R BT.1886 (2011)**, which specifies an
approximately 2.4 power function accounting for display black level.

Critically, the two are *not* inverses of one another. The deliberate ~1.2 end-to-end system
gamma (the OOTF) is what makes Rec.709 material look correct in a dim viewing environment.
Collapsing both into a single "implied gamma" is the single most common source of confusion in
this area, and a handbook that elsewhere argues passionately for precise terminology
(see *[Video ≠ Linear](digital-intermediates.md#video-linear)*) should be precise here too.

**Suggested replacement:** "Rec.709 defines the HDTV color primaries, white point, and a
camera-side OETF with a 0.45 power segment; it does not define display behaviour. The
reference display EOTF for Rec.709 material is given by BT.1886, approximately a 2.4 power law.
There is a deliberate end-to-end system gamma of roughly 1.2 between the two, appropriate to a
dim viewing environment — the OETF and EOTF are not inverses and should not be described as
one 'implied gamma'."

### CinemaScope aspect ratio history

> **In [Resolutions](resolutions.md#scope):** "CinemaScope began as 2.35:1, but in an effort to
> better hide frame splices camera manufacturers moved to 2.39:1 and later 2.40:1."

Two problems:

1. **Wrong actor.** These were **SMPTE standards revisions**, not camera-manufacturer
   initiatives. The anamorphic aperture was standardized at 2.35:1 in *SMPTE PH22.106-1957*,
   revised to approximately 2.39:1 in the 1970 revision (*PH22.106-1971*), and reaffirmed in
   *SMPTE 195-1993*.
2. **2.40:1 was never a standard.** It is a colloquial rounding of 2.39:1. There is no
   2.35 → 2.39 → 2.40 progression. The DCI Scope container (2048x858) is 2.387:1, confirming
   2.39.

The splice-hiding rationale itself is genuine and worth keeping — a narrowed aperture does mask
the flash of negative-assembly splices at the frame edge.

**Suggested replacement:** "The anamorphic aperture was standardized by SMPTE at 2.35:1 in
1957 and revised to approximately 2.39:1 in 1970, in part to better conceal negative-assembly
splices; it was reaffirmed in SMPTE 195-1993. '2.40:1' is a common rounding of 2.39:1, not a
separate standard."

### Wipster described as "a Vimeo product"

> **In [VFX Production Management](vfx-production-management.md#asset-management):**
> "Wipster.io (a Vimeo product)".

Wipster is not, and never was, a Vimeo product. It is an independent, bootstrapped company
founded in 2013 in Wellington, New Zealand by Rollo Wenlock, and it is still operating
independently at wipster.io. The likely source of the confusion is that Vimeo hosted Wipster's
marketing videos, and Vimeo later built competing review tools.

**Action:** delete the Vimeo attribution.

### DCI X'Y'Z' encoding

> **In [Color Spaces](color.md#dci-xyz):** "implicitly encoded as gamma 2.6 with an additional
> compensation factor to increase the range of producible white points."

Directionally right but vague, and the stated reason is slightly off. The encoding is:

```
CV  = round(4095 × (XYZ / 52.37)^(1/2.6))
XYZ = 52.37 × (CV / 4095)^2.6
```

with XYZ in absolute cd/m² and reference white at 48 cd/m². The normalizing constant
**52.37 = 48 × (4095/3960)^2.6** — the 4095/3960 code-value ratio is the origin of the headroom.
So the "compensation factor" is approximately 9% luminance headroom above the 48 cd/m²
reference white, which is what allows non-D-cinema white points (D55, D65) and
brighter-than-reference values to remain encodable. It is not really about "more white points"
per se.

**Action:** replace the vague phrase with the explicit derivation.

is now almost entirely legacy. Notable changes:

| 2017 entry | 2026 status |

| RED (R3D) | R3D still current; **RED was acquired by Nikon**, completed 8 April 2024. DSMC2/WEAPON/RANGER discontinued Dec 2022; original KOMODO discontinued Dec 2025. Current: V-RAPTOR [X] / XL / XE, KOMODO-X |

| Blackmagic (CinemaDNG) | **Wrong for 2026.** CinemaDNG was removed from Blackmagic cameras in the Camera 6.2 update; replaced by **Blackmagic RAW (BRAW)**, announced IBC September 2018 |

Additional items for a v1.1 camera section:

- **LogC4 / ARRI Wide Gamut 4**, introduced with the ALEXA 35 (2022, ALEV 4 sensor, 17 stops).
  LogC4 is EI-independent, with mid-grey at roughly 28% code value (1140/4095) versus LogC3's
  ~39%. AWG4 is larger than Rec.2020 but sits fully inside ACES AP0 to limit virtual colors. Part
  of ARRI REVEAL color science. LogC3/AWG3 remains correct for ALEXA Mini, AMIRA, and
  ALEXA LF/Mini LF.
- **ARRICORE**, announced 31 July 2025 with the ALEXA 35 Xtreme — an RGB (not Bayer) codec at
  roughly half ARRIRAW's data rate, with sensitivity, white balance, and tint still not baked in.

**House style:** the company brands itself **ARRI** (all caps), with products styled ALEXA,
ARRIRAW, AMIRA, ARRICORE. The 2017 text uses "Arri" and "Alexa" throughout. This port has
normalized to ARRI/ALEXA; confirm that matches your preference.

### Codecs

- **ProRes RAW** (2018) postdates this edition and should be covered. In 2026 it is recorded
  in-camera and via Atomos recorders; Windows support requires Apple's separate decoder
  package.
- **AV1** is the significant new distribution codec — roughly 91.5% real-world decoder support
  for Profile 0 8-bit, and Netflix passed 30% of streaming hours on AV1 in December 2025.
  Royalty-free, with native decode in all major browsers.
- **H.266/VVC** is niche by comparison — around 5% consumer hardware-decode footprint, no
  browser support. Its real traction is broadcast, notably Brazil's TV 3.0 / DTV+, which mandates
  VVC and launched commercial 4K HDR over-the-air in August 2025.
- **OpenEXR** is now at 3.4.x. Changes worth citing since 2017: the OpenEXRCore C API (3.1) for
  thread-safe non-blocking access; DWAA/DWAB lossy compression; HTJ2K lossless compression
  (3.4); ID Manifest attributes (3.0); and new standard camera/lens/production metadata
  attributes including `colorInteropID` (3.2). Note that the **ACES container is a constrained
  subset of EXR** — historically only `NO_COMPRESSION`, `PIZ`, and `B44A` are permitted, so DWA
  is *not* legal in an ACES-compliant EXR. That is a practical trap worth a callout.
- **Avid DNx**: at IBC 2025 Avid rebranded the family to simply "Avid DNx", dropping the
  HD/HR/GX suffixes in favour of the five quality levels. The 2025 revision of SMPTE ST 2019-1
  (VC-3) folded most of DNx GX into the core spec.
- The Apple ProRes white paper URL cited in the original still resolves.

### Rec.2020 display coverage

The 2017 claim that few displays can reproduce Rec.2020 is **still substantially accurate** — no
consumer or reference display covers the full gamut, and high-end 2025–26 OLED and mini-LED
panels measure roughly 75–85% BT.2020 while hitting ~99% DCI-P3.

What the passage lacks is the practical nuance: Rec.2020 functions as a **container** gamut. HDR
is mastered in **P3-D65 limited inside a Rec.2100/Rec.2020 encoding**, not graded to true
Rec.2020 primaries.

### Univisium and 2.00:1

The [Univisium reference](resolutions.md#high-definition) is accurate — 2.00:1 is indeed
Storaro's advocated format, and *Jurassic World* (2015) was shot and released 2.00:1 (the DP
wanted 2.40, Spielberg pushed for 1.85 headroom for the dinosaurs, and they compromised at
2.00:1 inside a 1.85 DCP container).

What needs updating is the framing: 2.00:1 is now mainstream across streaming originals, not a
recent indie curiosity. Worth noting too that *Fallen Kingdom* (2018) moved to true 2.39
anamorphic.

## Gaps

### ACES and OpenColorIO

**This is the most significant gap in the document.** The 2017 edition barely mentions ACES and
does not mention OpenColorIO at all. In 2026, both are baseline literacy for anyone running a
VFX or DI pipeline, and a handbook aimed at teaching color-managed workflow to independent
productions is materially deficient without them.

A v1.1 should add a dedicated chapter covering at minimum:

**ACES** — now developed under the Academy Software Foundation (repo `aces-aswf/aces`,
formerly `ampas/aces-dev`). Version history: 1.0 (Dec 2014), 1.1 (2018), 1.2 (2020),
1.3 (Apr 2021), and **2.0, released 4 April 2025**.

Components to name:

| Component | Role |
| --- | --- |
| **ACES2065-1** | AP0 primaries, linear — the archival/interchange space (SMPTE ST 2065-1) |
| **ACEScg** | AP1, scene-linear — the VFX/CG working space |
| **ACEScc / ACEScct** | AP1 log grading spaces; ACEScct adds a film-like toe near black |
| **Input Transform** | Formerly IDT |
| **Output Transform** | Formerly RRT + ODT, concatenated since ACES 1.1 |
| **LMT** | Look Modification Transform |

**ACES 2.0's key change:** the separate RRT + ODT model is gone, replaced by a single Output
Transform built on the Hellwig 2022 JMh color appearance model, with a unified tone scale,
chroma compression, and volumetric gamut compression — plus much improved invertibility.

!!! warning "Worth an explicit caution in v1.1"
    ACES 2.0 output is **not** a drop-in match for 1.x. Shows already in flight should not switch
    versions mid-production. This is exactly the kind of pipeline trap the handbook exists to warn
    about.

**OpenColorIO** — currently 2.5.x, and specified as such by the **VFX Reference Platform CY2026**
(which also specifies OpenEXR 3.4.x). OCIO is the de facto color-management layer across Nuke,
Maya, Houdini, Blender, and Katana. In practice ACES now reaches facilities primarily as **OCIO
built-in configs** (`studio-config` / `cg-config`) rather than as raw CTL — which is the practical
detail an independent production actually needs.

### HDR mastering

The document's only HDR content is a passing reference to "EDR (extended dynamic range,
theatrical) and HDR (high dynamic range, home video)" in the
[Camera Log](digital-intermediates.md#camera-log) section. That framing is not how the industry
describes it in 2026, and "EDR" is legacy vendor language that should be dropped or explicitly
flagged as such.

A v1.1 needs a real HDR section covering:

- **ITU-R BT.2100** — Rec.2020 primaries, D65, 10-bit minimum (12-bit for mastering), with two
  transfer functions: **PQ** (SMPTE ST 2084, absolute, up to 10,000 nits) and **HLG** (relative,
  SDR-backward-compatible, used for live broadcast).
- Distribution formats built on PQ: **HDR10** (static ST 2086 / MaxCLL / MaxFALL metadata),
  **HDR10+** (dynamic), and **Dolby Vision** (dynamic, with trim passes).
- Typical mastering practice: PQ at 1000 or 4000 nits in P3-D65 limited inside a Rec.2100
  container, with an SDR Rec.709 trim.
- **Dolby Vision Cinema / Dolby Cinema** as the theatrical HDR presentation, with dramatically
  higher contrast and peak luminance than the 48 cd/m² DCI standard.

### IMF

[IMF is covered in three sentences](production-workflow.md#imf-interoperable-master-format). In
2026 it is the dominant streaming and OTT mastering-and-delivery format and deserves
proportionate treatment.

Specifics for a v1.1: IMF is the **SMPTE ST 2067** family. **Netflix requires IMF App #2E**,
specifically ST 2067-21:2016, :2020, or :2023, with immersive audio per ST 2067-201:2019 (IAB).
The section should explain the component model — **CPL** (composition playlist), **PKL**,
**ASSETMAP**, track files, and **supplemental packages** for versioning and localization — since
that component/supplemental structure is precisely why IMF displaced flat ProRes and DPX
masters.

### Other candidates for new material

Not researched in depth, but worth considering for scope:

- **Remote and cloud workflows.** The 2017 text treats virtual/distributed teams as an
  independent-production compromise. Post-2020 this is mainstream practice at every budget
  level, with an established toolset (Teradici/HP Anyware, Jump Desktop, ClearView Flex,
  Sohonet, cloud-hosted Resolve and Nuke).
- **Virtual production and LED volumes.** Barely existed in 2017; now a major on-set VFX
  discipline with direct consequences for color pipeline and plate handling.
- **Machine-learning tooling** in roto, paint, upscaling, and depth estimation.
- **Camera-to-cloud** workflows (Frame.io C2C and equivalents), which change the dailies
  chapter's assumptions.
