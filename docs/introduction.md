# Introduction

Digital intermediate finishing and visual effects are staples of the modern Hollywood movie.
Visual effects are no longer the sole province of studio blockbusters, but are prevalent at all
levels of production. Independent filmmakers are now often able to accomplish more on their
laptops in a café than could be completed in a visual effects studio thirty years ago.

The changing landscape of digital technology has been empowering and has opened many new and
exciting doors to filmmakers.

## Why is this document necessary?

The majority of independent productions do not produce visual effects on the same scale,
quantity, or budget that major studio productions do. In many independent productions, visual
effects artists are freelance hires and, depending on the scale of the project, there may or may
not be a VFX supervisor. More often than not, there is no VFX producer, VFX coordinator, or
VFX production manager. Independent features are lucky to have a post-production supervisor.
Oftentimes these roles are combined and tasked to the resident post-production expert — the
picture editor.

Post-production, like production, can be expensive if poorly managed. Providing a clean and
efficient workflow from production through editorial and post-production finishing is key to
maximizing available budget and resources. Organization and communication between
departments is essential.

It is common in independent productions for a colorist to be brought onto a project towards
the end of post-production, often after much of the visual effects work has been completed.
This places the colorist in the precarious position of inheriting a workflow in which they have had
no input.

It is not unheard of for independent films to have performed visual effects work in a non-ideal
color space, resolution, or format that adversely affects the image quality. Technical issues can
prevent visual effects shots from living up to their potential. These issues can often be
addressed early on in the production process if relevant organization and communication
efforts are observed.

At a late stage, it is often too financially burdensome to undo mistakes. Understanding color
workflow and instigating a managed visual effects pipeline is critical to the success of
independent and studio projects alike. Filmmaking is about the art, but the art can't happen
unless the groundwork is laid properly. By planning ahead, more of a production's budget can
be spent on adding quality, rather than fixing mistakes.

## Intended Audience

This document is intended for independent producers, editors, and freelance visual effects
artists interested in adapting studio-like practices in digital intermediates and visual effects to
their independent productions. This document focuses on aspects often foreign to first-time
feature filmmakers, but will benefit experienced filmmakers as well. Familiarity with
post-production workflow, digital intermediates, and computer graphics is encouraged.
